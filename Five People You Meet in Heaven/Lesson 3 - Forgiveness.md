# Lesson 3: Forgiveness

> [!note] The Lesson
> *"Holding anger is a poison. It eats you from inside. We think that hating is a weapon that attacks the person who harmed us. But hatred is a curved blade. And the harm we do, we do to ourselves."*

## Teacher

**[[Ruby]]** — The namesake of Ruby Pier

## Context

Eddie's third heaven is a snowy mountain with a diner. Inside, he sees his father — silent, smoking, ignoring him, even in death. An old woman appears: Ruby, the woman whose face once adorned the pier's entrance.

Ruby reveals she shared a hospital room with Eddie's father. She saw his final nights. She tells Eddie the truth about his father's death.

## The Lesson Explained

### 1. Anger Is Self-Poisoning
Ruby uses the metaphor of a curved blade: when you swing hatred at someone, the blade curves back and cuts you.

### 2. Eddie's Father Was Not Evil
Eddie's father was neglectful and violent, yes. But he also:
- Saved Mickey Shea from drowning, knowing Mickey had attacked his wife
- Died from loyalty, not drunkenness
- In his final night, called out for his family

### 3. Forgiveness Frees the Forgiver
Eddie didn't need to forgive his father for his father's sake. He needed to forgive for his own. His anger trapped him at Ruby Pier just as much as his father's death did.

### 4. The Act of Forgiveness
Eddie kneels at his father's booth. He says:
> *"I was angry with you, Dad. I hated you... But you're my father. I'll let it go now, all right?"*

He pounds the table, sobs, and finally whispers the words that defined their relationship:
> *"It's fixed."*

## Real-World Application

- Forgiveness does not require reconciliation or condoning abuse.
- It requires understanding the full context of someone's actions.
- The primary beneficiary of forgiveness is the person who forgives.

## Key Quote

> *"Forgive, Edward. Forgive. Do you remember the lightness you felt when you first arrived in heaven? That's because no one is born with anger. And when we die, the soul is freed of it. But now, here, in order to move on, you must understand why you felt what you did, and why you no longer need to feel it."*
