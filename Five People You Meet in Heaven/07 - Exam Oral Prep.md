# 07 - Exam Oral Prep

This note is designed for **oral exam preparation** where you must speak for **3–5 minutes** per answer. The examiner asks open-ended questions that require opinion, evidence, and reflection — but crucially, you must also **introduce the book** and **weave in personal experience**.

For each question type below, you will find:

- **What the examiner is really testing**
- **A timing breakdown** for your 3–5 minute response
- **A framework for answering** (so you don't freeze)
- **Evidence you can cite** from the book
- **A full sample answer** showing book summary + personal experience woven together

> [!tip] The Golden Structure for 3–5 Minutes
> Your answer should have **three natural parts**:
> 1. **Book Context (45–60 sec)** — Briefly explain what the book is about, who Eddie is, and which theme/character relates to the question. This proves you know the text.
> 2. **Your Answer + Evidence + Personal Experience (2–3 min)** — State your opinion, support it with a specific scene or quote, then bridge to your own life. *"This reminds me of when..."* or *"I have experienced something similar..."*
> 3. **Reflection / Conclusion (30–45 sec)** — Summarize why this matters to you, what you learned, or how the book changed your view.
>
> This is the **PEEL-P method**: Point, Evidence, Explanation, Link, **Personal**.

## Your 45-Second Book Introduction (Use This to Start ANY Answer)

Memorize a flexible introduction like this. Adapt the last sentence to match the question:

> *"The Five People You Meet in Heaven by Mitch Albom is about an elderly man named Eddie who works as a maintenance worker at an amusement park called Ruby Pier. He dies saving a little girl from a falling ride cart, and in heaven he meets five people who explain the hidden meaning of his life. Through these meetings, Eddie learns about connection, sacrifice, forgiveness, love, and purpose — lessons that transform his understanding of his 'ordinary' life. The question you asked me relates to this because..."*

**Why this works:**
- It shows you understand the plot, setting, protagonist, and themes.
- It takes about 45 seconds at normal speaking pace.
- The final sentence is your pivot to the actual question.

> [!tip] Adapt the last sentence:
> - *"...relates to this because the book made me think deeply about friendship and which character I would want by my side."*
> - *"...relates to this because the ending is controversial, and I have my own opinion about it."*
> - *"...relates to this because one lesson in particular changed how I see my own life."*

---

## How to Weave Personal Experience Naturally

The examiner wants to hear **you** in the answer — not just a book report. Here are four natural bridges from the text to your life:

### Bridge 1: "This reminds me of..."
> *"When Dominguez grieves for Eddie, it reminds me of how I felt when my grandfather retired. The house felt quieter, just like the maintenance shop felt quieter to Dominguez."*

### Bridge 2: "I have experienced something similar..."
> *"Eddie believes his life is meaningless until Tala shows him otherwise. I have experienced something similar: last year I volunteered at a food bank and thought my work was unimportant, until a recipient told me it was the only meal her family had that week."*

### Bridge 3: "This changed how I see..."
> *"Marguerite's lesson that 'lost love is still love' changed how I see my relationship with my cousin who moved abroad. We don't talk every day, but the love is still there, transformed into memory and occasional messages."*

### Bridge 4: "If I were in Eddie's position..."
> *"If I were in Eddie's position, I don't know if I could forgive my father the way he eventually does. But the book made me try. Recently, I had an argument with my mother, and instead of holding a grudge, I remembered Ruby's words about the curved blade — and I apologized first."*

> [!warning] What NOT to do
> - Don't invent a tragic story just to sound deep. Examiners can tell.
> - Don't spend more time on your life than on the book. Aim for a **60/40 split** (book/personal).
> - Don't say "This relates to my life because..." every time. Use the bridges above for variety.

## Type 1: "Who would you choose to be your classmate / friend?"

### Timing Breakdown
| Part | Time | What to do |
|------|------|------------|
| Book intro | 45 sec | Explain Eddie, Ruby Pier, the five lessons |
| Your choice + evidence | 90 sec | Name the character, describe a scene, explain their traits |
| Personal experience | 60 sec | Connect to a friend you have, or a trait you value |
| Reflection | 45 sec | Why this character would make school/life better |

### What the examiner wants
- Can you analyze a character's personality?
- Can you support your choice with evidence?
- Can you connect character traits to real-life friendship?

### Strong Candidates

| Character | Why | Evidence to Use |
|-----------|-----|-----------------|
| **Dominguez** | Loyal, optimistic, kind to elders | Wishes Eddie happy birthday; devastated at his death; inherits his job with respect |
| **Marguerite** | Warm, forgiving, sees the best in people | Dances with Eddie despite his gruffness; forgives him for gambling; says "You loved me anyway" |
| **Eddie (young)** | Hardworking, protective of others | Defends his brother; fixes rides to keep children safe; makes pipe-cleaner animals for kids |

### Weak Choices (and how to defend them if you pick them)
| Character | Risk | How to defend it |
|-----------|------|------------------|
| **The Captain** | Military, strict | "I admire his loyalty and self-sacrifice. He kept his promise: no one gets left behind." |
| **Eddie (old)** | Grumpy, bitter | "I would befriend him *because* he is misunderstood. Under the gruffness, he is deeply caring." |

### Full Sample Answer (3.5–4 minutes)

> *"The Five People You Meet in Heaven by Mitch Albom is about an elderly man named Eddie who works as a maintenance worker at an amusement park called Ruby Pier. He dies saving a little girl from a falling ride cart, and in heaven he meets five people who explain the hidden meaning of his life. Through these meetings, Eddie learns about connection, sacrifice, forgiveness, love, and purpose — lessons that transform his understanding of his 'ordinary' life. The question you asked me relates to this because the book made me think deeply about friendship and which character I would want by my side.*
>
> *If I had to choose one character from the book to be my classmate, I would choose **Dominguez**. Dominguez is a young maintenance worker at Ruby Pier who works alongside Eddie. What I admire most about him is his loyalty and kindness. In the book, when Eddie dies, Dominguez is absolutely devastated. He comes to work early the next morning, turns on the sink in the maintenance shop, and then turns it off again because the silence without Eddie is unbearable. That small detail shows he didn't just see Eddie as a coworker — he truly cared about him as a person. He also respects Eddie's legacy by packing his things into a trunk and leaving them 'where they were, as if he were coming back tomorrow.'*
>
> *This reminds me of my own experience with a mentor I had last year — an older student who helped me adjust to a new school. When they graduated, I felt the same way Dominguez did: the hallways felt quieter, and I realized how much their daily presence had meant to me. Just like Dominguez inherited Eddie's job with respect, I tried to continue the traditions they started, like welcoming new students.*
>
> *I would want Dominguez as a classmate because he represents the kind of friend who notices you, remembers you, and honors you even when you are gone. In a world where people often move on too quickly, Dominguez stays. That is the kind of loyalty I value most in friendship."*

### What the examiner wants
- Can you analyze a character's personality?
- Can you support your choice with evidence?
- Can you connect character traits to real-life friendship?

> *"I would choose **Dominguez** to be my classmate because he is loyal and kind-hearted. In the book, when Eddie dies, Dominguez is devastated. He comes to work early the next morning and turns on the sink, then turns it off because the silence without Eddie is unbearable. This shows he truly valued Eddie, not just as a coworker but as a person. He also respects Eddie's legacy by keeping his things 'where they were, as if he were coming back tomorrow.' I would want a friend who remembers me that way."*

## Type 2: "How would you change the ending?"

### Timing Breakdown
| Part | Time | What to do |
|------|------|------------|
| Book intro | 45 sec | Explain Eddie's death and the five heaven meetings |
| Describe current ending | 45 sec | Show you understand why Albom ended it this way |
| Your change + evidence | 90 sec | Propose an alternative and support it with a scene |
| Personal experience | 60 sec | Connect to a time you wished you could change an ending |
| Reflection | 45 sec | Would your change improve or weaken the message? |

### What the examiner wants
- Do you understand the current ending's purpose?
- Can you imagine alternatives while respecting the book's themes?
- Can you argue whether your change improves or weakens the message?

### The Actual Ending (for reference)
Eddie dies saving Amy/Annie, meets five people in heaven, learns his life had purpose, and waits in line to meet Amy/Annie when she dies.

### Full Sample Answer (3.5–4 minutes)

> *"The Five People You Meet in Heaven by Mitch Albom is about an elderly man named Eddie who works as a maintenance worker at an amusement park called Ruby Pier. He dies saving a little girl from a falling ride cart, and in heaven he meets five people who explain the hidden meaning of his life. Through these meetings, Eddie learns about connection, sacrifice, forgiveness, love, and purpose — lessons that transform his understanding of his 'ordinary' life. The question you asked me relates to this because the ending is one of the most debated parts of the book, and I have my own opinion about it.*
>
> *First, I want to explain why the current ending is powerful. Eddie dies believing his life was wasted, but in heaven he discovers it had deep meaning. He meets Tala, the child he accidentally killed in the war, and she tells him that his maintenance job at Ruby Pier kept thousands of children safe. This gives him peace. The book ends with Eddie waiting in line to meet Amy — the girl he saved — when she eventually dies. It is a beautiful circle: Eddie, who felt invisible on Earth, becomes someone else's teacher in heaven.*
>
> *However, if I could change the ending, I would add one more scene on Earth after Eddie's death. I would show Ruby Pier ten years later, with a small plaque near Freddy's Free Fall that reads: 'In memory of Eddie, who kept us safe.' Children would ride the carousel, and Dominguez — now the head maintenance worker — would tighten a bolt the way Eddie taught him. This would make Eddie's purpose visible to the world, not just to him in heaven.*
>
> *I want this because of something that happened in my own life. My grandmother worked as a cleaner for thirty years, and when she retired, nobody threw a party or gave her a plaque. She felt invisible. Only years later did I learn how many people relied on her quiet work. Reading about Eddie made me think of her — and I wish the world had recognized her while she was still here.*
>
> *That said, I understand why Albom didn't write it this way. The book's message is that ordinary lives have meaning *even without* recognition. If Eddie got a plaque, it might suggest that purpose only counts when others see it. So while my change would feel emotionally satisfying, Albom's ending is probably more honest — and more challenging."*

### Alternative Approaches (if you want something different)

#### Approach B: Eddie Meets a Sixth Person
> *"I would add a sixth person: Amy/Annie as an adult. She could tell Eddie how his sacrifice allowed her to become a mother or a teacher. This would make the connection theme even stronger — Eddie's death created a life he never saw."*
> **Evidence:** The epilogue says Amy/Annie will meet five people; Nicky's lost key caused the accident; Tala's lesson about invisible connections.

#### Approach C: Make Eddie Live (harder to defend)
> *"I would change the ending so Eddie survives but is severely injured. He would finally receive recognition from the park owners. However, this might weaken Albom's message that ordinary lives have meaning even without recognition."*
> **Evidence:** Eddie's feeling of invisibility; Dominguez's grief; the park reopening without ceremony.

### What NOT to say
- "I would make Eddie rich and famous." (Misses the point — the book criticizes worldly success.)
- "I would let Eddie and Marguerite live happily together." (Ignores that her death is central to Lesson 4.)
- "I would remove the war scenes." (They are essential to sacrifice and purpose.)

## Type 3: "What is the most important lesson in the book?"

### Timing Breakdown
| Part | Time | What to do |
|------|------|------------|
| Book intro | 45 sec | Briefly introduce Eddie and the five lessons |
| State your choice + evidence | 90 sec | Pick one lesson, explain it with a scene and quote |
| Personal experience | 60 sec | Connect to your own life — a time you felt this lesson |
| Reflection | 45 sec | Why this lesson matters in today's world |

### What the examiner wants
- Can you evaluate and rank themes?
- Can you argue why one lesson matters more than the others?
- Can you connect it to the real world and your own experience?

### Full Sample Answer: Lesson 3 — Forgiveness (3.5–4 minutes)

> *"The Five People You Meet in Heaven by Mitch Albom is about an elderly man named Eddie who works as a maintenance worker at an amusement park called Ruby Pier. He dies saving a little girl from a falling ride cart, and in heaven he meets five people who explain the hidden meaning of his life. Through these meetings, Eddie learns about connection, sacrifice, forgiveness, love, and purpose — lessons that transform his understanding of his 'ordinary' life. The question you asked me relates to this because one lesson in particular changed how I see my own relationships.*
>
> *While all five lessons are important, I believe **forgiveness** is the most important. Eddie spends his whole life angry at his father — a silent, violent man who neglected him and never said 'I love you.' Eddie even grabs his father's arm when he returns from the war, and his father never speaks to him again. This anger poisons Eddie. It traps him at Ruby Pier, makes him bitter, and prevents him from feeling joy.*
>
> *In heaven, Ruby teaches Eddie the truth: his father didn't die from drunkenness — he died saving Mickey Shea from drowning, even though Mickey had attacked Eddie's mother. Ruby says, 'Holding anger is a poison. It eats you from inside. We think that hating is a weapon that attacks the person who harmed us. But hatred is a curved blade. And the harm we do, we do to ourselves.' When Eddie finally kneels and whispers 'It's fixed,' he is freed.*
>
> *This lesson hit me personally because I had a conflict with a close friend two years ago. We stopped talking over something that, looking back, was not worth losing the friendship. I carried anger for months, and it made me anxious and unhappy — exactly like Ruby describes. Reading this book made me realize I was hurting myself more than I was hurting my friend. I eventually reached out, and while we are not as close as before, the weight lifted immediately.*
>
> *I think forgiveness is the most important lesson because we live in a world where people hold grudges for years — online, in families, between countries. Albom shows that forgiveness is not weakness; it is survival. Without it, Eddie would have stayed trapped forever. And without it, many of us stay trapped in our own pain."*

### How to Defend the Other Lessons (quick frameworks)

| Lesson | Your Argument | Personal Angle |
|--------|---------------|----------------|
| **Connection** | Foundation of all other lessons | A time you realized a stranger affected your life |
| **Sacrifice** | The entire plot depends on it | A parent, teacher, or friend who sacrificed for you |
| **Love** | The only thing that survives death | Losing someone and learning to keep their memory alive |
| **Purpose** | Answers the central question "Did my life matter?" | Feeling invisible in a task, then realizing it mattered |

> [!tip] Pro Move
> Pick the lesson you can argue most passionately. Then say: *"While all five lessons are important, [Lesson X] is the most important because the other lessons would not be possible without it."*

## Type 4: "Which character do you relate to the most?"

### What the examiner wants
- Self-reflection
- Ability to connect literature to personal experience
- Understanding of character complexity

### Sample Framework

1. **Name the character** (be specific — "young Eddie" vs. "old Eddie" are different people)
2. **Name the shared experience** (feeling invisible, losing someone, family conflict)
3. **Cite the evidence** (a scene or quote)
4. **Explain what you learned** (how the book changed your perspective)

### Example

> *"I relate most to **Dominguez** because I have often felt like the younger person looking up to someone older and wiser. Dominguez admires Eddie, asks him about fishing, and inherits his job with respect. But he also shows that he cares deeply — he is devastated when Eddie dies. This reminds me that we should tell people we appreciate them before it is too late."*

### Another Example

> *"I relate most to **Eddie when he feels his life is meaningless** because I sometimes worry that my own small actions don't matter. Eddie thinks fixing rides is a wasted life, but Tala reveals he kept thousands of children safe. This book taught me that purpose is not about being famous — it is about being faithful in small things."*

## Type 5: "Would you recommend this book? To whom?"

### What the examiner wants
- Can you identify the book's audience?
- Can you match themes to specific life situations?
- Can you be critical while still recommending it?

### Strong Answer

> *"I would recommend this book to anyone who has ever felt that their life is insignificant or who is grieving the loss of a loved one. Eddie's journey proves that ordinary lives have extraordinary meaning, and Marguerite's lesson that 'lost love is still love' offers real comfort. I would especially give it to someone working a 'thankless' job — a cleaner, a maintenance worker, a caregiver — because Tala's lesson shows that their work matters more than society recognizes."*

### If You Want to Add Criticism

> *"I would recommend it, but with one caution: the book is very sentimental. Some readers might find the heaven scenes too convenient or the metaphors too heavy. But for someone who needs hope or healing, the sentiment is exactly what makes it powerful."*

## Type 6: "What does the title mean?"

### What the examiner wants
- Literal understanding: Eddie meets five people in heaven
- Symbolic understanding: We all have five people who explain our lives
- Thematic understanding: Connection, closure, understanding

### Sample Answer

> *"The title works on three levels. Literally, it refers to Eddie meeting five people in heaven who explain his life. Symbolically, it suggests that everyone has five people — known or unknown — who shaped their destiny. Thematically, it means that no life is isolated. The Blue Man says, 'Strangers are just family you have yet to come to know.' The title promises that even after death, we will understand the meaning of our connections."*

## Type 7: "Compare two characters" (e.g., Eddie and his father)

### What the examiner wants
- Analytical comparison, not just description
- Identification of similarities AND differences
- Evidence for both sides

### Framework: Venn Diagram in Words

| Similarities | Eddie | Eddie's Father |
|--------------|-------|----------------|
| Both worked at Ruby Pier | Felt trapped by the job | Also felt trapped by life |
| Both were silent about pain | Never told Marguerite his full feelings | Never told Eddie he loved him |
| Both sacrificed | Gave up his dreams | Gave up his health saving Mickey |
| Both were misunderstood | Thought his life was wasted | Thought his son was ungrateful |

### Sample Answer

> *"Eddie and his father are more alike than Eddie realizes. Both men worked at Ruby Pier their whole lives, both were silent about their suffering, and both sacrificed without being understood. The difference is that Eddie gets to learn the truth in heaven, while his father died without reconciliation. This makes the book's message about forgiveness even more urgent — we must forgive before it is too late."*

## Type 8: "What did you learn from this book?"

### What the examiner wants
- Personal growth
- Connection to themes
- Specific evidence, not vague feelings

### Sample Answer Skeletons

**About self-worth:**
> *"I learned that my 'small' actions matter more than I think. Eddie tightened bolts and checked brakes every day, thinking it was meaningless. Tala showed him that those actions saved thousands of children. This made me rethink my own daily routines — whether I am kind to a classmate or help at home, it might have an invisible impact I will never see."*

**About forgiveness:**
> *"I learned that holding anger hurts me more than the person I am angry at. Ruby says hatred is 'a curved blade.' Eddie hated his father for decades, and it trapped him at Ruby Pier. Only in heaven could he let it go. I now try to resolve conflicts faster because I don't want to carry that poison."*

**About love:**
> *"I learned that love doesn't end when someone dies. Marguerite tells Eddie, 'Life has to end. Love doesn't.' This changed how I think about people I have lost. Instead of feeling that love is gone, I now see memory as a way of continuing the relationship."*

## Emergency Cheat Sheet: If Your Mind Goes Blank

1. **Pause.** Take a breath. The examiner expects thinking time.
2. **Pick one character** you remember well.
3. **Pick one scene** with that character.
4. **Say what happened** and **why it matters**.

Example (if asked anything and you freeze):
> *"I need a moment... OK. I want to talk about Eddie and Marguerite dancing at the Stardust Band Shell. This scene shows that Eddie was capable of joy and love, even though his life seemed sad later. It matters because it proves that even 'ordinary' people have beautiful moments that give their lives meaning."*

This answer works for: "Describe a key scene," "What is love in the book?," "Why does Eddie matter?," or "What is the mood of the novel?"
